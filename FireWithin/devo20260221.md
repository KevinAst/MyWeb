The apostle Paul reminds us that the hardships of this life are real,
but they are not ultimate. He does not deny suffering. In fact, he
endured imprisonment, persecution, and physical pain. Yet he calls
these trials “light affliction” and “but for a moment.” How could he
speak this way? Because he measured present trouble against eternal
glory.

Paul understood that this world is not our home. Our bodies are frail
and temporary, like tents that wear out with time. Our days here are
marked by weakness, sorrow, and the steady reminder that all earthly
things fade. But for those who belong to Christ, this is not the end
of the story. God has prepared something greater.

The same God who raised Jesus from the dead has promised to raise
us. What awaits the believer is not merely relief from suffering, but
the fullness of life in the presence of the Lord. The glory to come is
not symbolic language for improvement; it is a real and eternal
reality secured by Christ’s resurrection. Compared to that everlasting
inheritance, even a lifetime of hardship is brief.

This eternal perspective reshapes how we endure today. We do not
minimize pain, nor do we pretend difficulty does not hurt. But we
refuse to lose heart. Each trial is being used by God to prepare us
for glory. Every burden borne in faith deepens our longing for what is
permanent.

So we press on. We fix our eyes not on what is seen, but on what is
unseen. The visible is temporary. The unseen is eternal. And Christ
Himself is our assurance that the best is yet to come.
